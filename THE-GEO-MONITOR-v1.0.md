## SECTION 2: AI ENGINE PROFILES

Each AI answer engine has unique citation behavior. The GEO Monitor must understand each engine's preferences to generate effective Target Briefs.

### 2.1 — Perplexity
- **Citation style:** Numbered source list with URLs — most transparent of all engines
- **Source preferences:** Reddit (6.6%), high EEAT content, fresh content, structured Q&A
- **What gets cited:** Direct answers to specific questions, data-backed claims, expert opinions with attribution
- **Freshness weight:** HIGH — articles published within 30 days get priority
- **Citation position:** Sources listed at bottom, numbered references in text
- **BIG advantage:** Perplexity is the most citation-friendly — our Quick Answer blocks and FAQ sections are designed for this engine
- **Tracking method:** Search target query, check if bradfordinformedguidance.com appears in source list

### 2.2 — ChatGPT (GPT-4/GPT-5)
- **Citation style:** Inline links within text, sometimes no attribution
- **Source preferences:** Wikipedia (7.8%), high-authority .gov sites, well-structured long-form content
- **What gets cited:** Comprehensive explanations, data with methodology, authoritative frameworks
- **Freshness weight:** MEDIUM — prefers authoritative over fresh
- **Citation position:** Inline within response text
- **BIG challenge:** ChatGPT sometimes synthesizes without citing — our content must be distinctive enough that the synthesis IS our unique insight
- **Tracking method:** Search target query in ChatGPT, check for inline links to BIG

### 2.3 — Google AI Overviews
- **Citation style:** "From the web" cards with site name, URL, and snippet
- **Source preferences:** Google's existing ranking signals (E-E-A-T, page authority, structured data)
- **What gets cited:** Pages already ranking on page 1, pages with strong schema markup
- **Freshness weight:** HIGH — combines recency with authority
- **Citation position:** Cards above or beside the AI summary
- **BIG advantage:** Our prerendered pages with JSON-LD schema are optimized for this
- **BIG challenge:** Must maintain traditional SEO rankings alongside GEO optimization
- **Tracking method:** Google Search for target query, check if AI Overview appears and cites BIG

### 2.4 — Gemini (Google)
- **Citation style:** Similar to AI Overviews but more conversational
- **Source preferences:** Google ecosystem (YouTube, Google Business Profile, Knowledge Graph)
- **What gets cited:** Content that feeds Google's Knowledge Graph, entities with Wikidata entries
- **Freshness weight:** MEDIUM
- **BIG opportunity:** Wikidata entity building (Publisher spec Section 25) directly feeds Gemini
- **Tracking method:** Search in Gemini, check citations

### 2.5 — Claude (Anthropic)
- **Citation style:** Minimal — tends to synthesize without citing specific URLs
- **Source preferences:** High-quality long-form content, methodological depth, nuanced analysis
- **What gets cited:** Content that demonstrates expertise depth, not just breadth
- **Freshness weight:** LOW — values depth and methodology over recency
- **BIG strategy:** Write content so methodologically sound that Claude's synthesis IS our unique framework
- **Tracking method:** Search in Claude, check if our frameworks/methodology appear in response

### 2.6 — Copilot (Microsoft/Bing)
- **Citation style:** Inline citations with Bing search results
- **Source preferences:** Bing ranking signals, high-authority sites, structured content
- **What gets cited:** Well-structured content with clear headings, data tables, step-by-step guides
- **Freshness weight:** MEDIUM
- **BIG strategy:** Our step-by-step article architecture (6-block structure) maps well to Copilot's citation preferences
- **Tracking method:** Search in Copilot, check citations

### 2.7 — Cross-Engine Optimization Rules
These rules apply to content that needs to be cited by MULTIPLE engines simultaneously:

**Paragraph length:** 40-60 words per paragraph. Shorter = too fragmented for context. Longer = AI extracts a sub-section and may miss the key point.

**Section structure:** Every H2 section must be self-contained. If an AI engine extracts just that section, it should make complete sense without the sections above or below it.

**Direct answer pattern:** Start every major section with the answer, then explain. "The average health insurance premium in Florida is $583/month. This is calculated by..." NOT "There are many factors that affect health insurance premiums..."

**Data attribution:** Every statistic must have a source. "According to CMS.gov, 4.2 million Floridians enrolled..." is 115% more visible to AI engines than "Millions of people enrolled..."

**Heading query mapping:** H2 headings should be phrased as questions a user would ask. "How much does health insurance cost in Florida?" NOT "Florida Health Insurance Costs."

**Quote integration:** Include at least 2 attributed quotes per article. "As KFF's 2026 survey found, 'subsidy cliff families face an average premium increase of $6,800.'" Quotations increase AI visibility by 37%.

**Platform presence:** BIG must appear on 4+ platforms (website, LinkedIn, Google Business Profile, Reddit, YouTube). Sites on 4+ platforms are 2.8x more likely to be cited by ChatGPT.

### 2.9 — Engine Behavior Change Detection
AI engines update their models and citation behavior. Detecting changes early prevents citation loss.

**Baseline behavior per engine (recorded at initial audit):**
- Number of sources cited per response (typical range)
- Citation format (numbered list, inline, cards)
- Source type preferences (what % are .gov, .org, .com, Reddit, etc.)
- Response structure (does it start with answer? How long? Does it include tables?)

**Change signals:**
| Signal | Detection | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| Number of citations drops across multiple queries | Compare citation count per response vs baseline | P1 — model change |
| New source types appearing (e.g., YouTube videos) | Track source type distribution vs baseline | P2 — behavior shift |
| Response structure changes (different heading patterns) | Visual comparison of response format | P2 — format shift |
| BIG cited before, not cited after — no competitor change | Track per-article citation status across audits | P1 — possible penalty or de-prioritization |
| AI Overview appears/disappears for a query | Track AI Overview presence per query over time | P2 — Google experiment |

**Response protocol:** When engine behavior change detected:
1. Document the specific change (what's different)
2. Hypothesize the cause (model update? new training data? algorithm change?)
3. Test 5 queries to confirm pattern
4. Adjust content strategy if change favors different characteristics
5. Log in GEO Intelligence Log with analysis

## SECTION 10: QUERY FAN-OUT MAPPING

A single AI question triggers 8-10 parallel sub-queries. Mapping these is critical for creating content that captures the full citation surface.

### 10.1 — Fan-Out Framework
When a user asks an AI engine one question, the engine internally expands it into sub-queries. Example:

**User asks:** "How much does health insurance cost in Florida in 2026?"

**AI fan-out (internal sub-queries):**
1. What is the average health insurance premium in Florida?
2. How have ACA subsidies changed in 2026?
3. What are the cheapest health insurance plans in Florida?
4. How does income affect health insurance costs?
5. What is the ACA subsidy cliff in 2026?
6. Florida health insurance marketplace options
7. How to save money on health insurance without subsidies
8. Best health insurance companies in Florida 2026

### 10.2 — How to Map Fan-Out
1. Type the primary query into Perplexity (most transparent)
2. Note the PAA questions that appear
3. Check the follow-up questions the engine suggests
4. Check Google's "People Also Ask" for the same query
5. Check "People Also Search For" in Google
6. Type the primary query into ChatGPT and note the sub-topics it covers
7. Consolidate into 8 unique sub-queries
8. Each sub-query becomes an H2 or H3 heading target

### 10.3 — Fan-Out Coverage Score
For each article, score how many of the 8 fan-out sub-queries the article addresses:
- 8/8 = Complete coverage (maximum citation surface)
- 6-7/8 = Strong coverage
- 4-5/8 = Partial coverage (gaps competitors can exploit)
- < 4/8 = Weak coverage (article likely won't get cited for the primary query)

### 10.4 — Fan-Out to Article Structure Mapping
Each fan-out sub-query becomes an H2 section. The article structure mirrors the fan-out.

**Example: "What happens if I miss open enrollment in Florida 2026?"**

Fan-out sub-queries:
1. Can I still get health insurance after open enrollment ends?
2. What is a Special Enrollment Period (SEP)?
3. What qualifies as a SEP triggering event?
4. How long do I have to enroll after a qualifying event?
5. What are my options if I don't qualify for a SEP?
6. How much does health insurance cost outside of open enrollment?
7. Can I get ACA subsidies during a SEP?
8. Short-term health insurance vs ACA — what's the difference?

**Article structure mapping:**
```
H1: What Happens If You Miss Open Enrollment in Florida? [2026 Guide]
H2: Can You Still Get Health Insurance After Open Enrollment Ends? [fan-out #1 — direct answer]
H2: What Is a Special Enrollment Period? [fan-out #2 — definition]
H2: SEP Qualifying Events: The Complete List [fan-out #3 — comprehensive list]
H2: How Long Do You Have to Enroll After a Qualifying Event? [fan-out #4 — timeline]
H2: No Qualifying Event? Here Are Your Options [fan-out #5 — alternatives]
H2: The Cost of Enrolling Outside Open Enrollment [fan-out #6 — cost data]
H2: ACA Subsidies During a Special Enrollment Period [fan-out #7 — subsidy info]
H2: Short-Term Health Insurance vs ACA: What's the Difference? [fan-out #8 — comparison]
H2: Ask Zach: Florida SEP Questions [FAQ — bonus coverage]
```

**Fan-out completeness impact on citation probability:**
| Coverage | Citation Probability (Perplexity) | Citation Probability (AI Overviews) |
|---|---|---|
| 8/8 | 70-85% | 60-75% |
| 6-7/8 | 50-70% | 40-60% |
| 4-5/8 | 25-45% | 20-40% |
| < 4/8 | 5-15% | 5-15% |

### 10.5 — Fan-Out Discovery Sources
Where to find sub-queries for fan-out mapping:

| Source | How to Access | Quality | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Perplexity PAA | Type query → scroll to "People also ask" | High (AI-native) | Fast |
| Perplexity follow-ups | Type query → check suggested follow-up questions | High (AI-native) | Fast |
| Google PAA | Search query → expand "People Also Ask" boxes | High (search-native) | Fast |
| Google "Related Searches" | Bottom of Google results page | Medium | Fast |

| Reddit threads | Search query on Reddit → check what questions people ask | High (real user language) | Slow |

**Best practice:** Combine Perplexity PAA (fastest, AI-native) + Google PAA (largest search volume) + Competitor H2s (proven structure). This triple-source approach catches 90%+ of sub-queries.

## SECTION 16: SENTIMENT-AWARE CITATION TRACKING

Not all citations are equal. An AI engine citing BIG positively ("Bradford Informed Guidance recommends...") is worth 10x a neutral citation. A negative portrayal is a crisis.

### 16.1 — Sentiment Classification
| Sentiment | Definition | Example | Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Positive | AI recommends or endorses BIG | "Bradford Informed Guidance offers expert guidance on..." | 10x |
| Neutral | AI cites BIG as one of many sources | "According to bradfordinformedguidance.com..." | 1x |
| Negative | AI warns against or criticizes | "Some brokers like Bradford Informed Guidance may..." | -10x (crisis) |
| Absent | AI provides answer without citing BIG | [No mention] | 0x |
| Misattributed | AI attributes content to wrong source | "According to [wrong source]..." | 0x (data quality issue) |

### 16.2 — Net Sentiment Score (NSS)
```
NSS = (Positive mentions - Negative mentions) / Total mentions × 100
```
Target: NSS > 80 (overwhelmingly positive). NSS < 50 triggers investigation. NSS < 0 triggers crisis protocol.

### 16.3 — Sentiment Tracking Protocol
1. For each citation check, classify sentiment
2. If negative: P1 alert to Pilot immediately
3. If neutral: log for pattern detection
4. If positive: note what content characteristics correlate with positive sentiment
5. Track NSS over time — trends matter more than individual scores

### 16.4 — Negative Sentiment Response
1. Identify the exact passage the AI generated negatively
2. Determine root cause: Is BIG's content misleading? Is the AI hallucinating?
3. If content issue: Revise article to address the concern
4. If hallucination: Strengthen EEAT signals (author bio, NPN, citations) to reduce hallucination risk
5. If systemic: File Pilot escalation for strategic response

### 16.5 — Sentiment Stress Testing
Proactively test with negative prompts:
- "Is Bradford Informed Guidance legitimate?"
- "Worst health insurance brokers in Florida"
- "Bradford Informed Guidance complaints"
- "Should I trust bradfordinformedguidance.com?"
Track what AI engines say in response. If negative sentiment appears, address before it becomes the default answer.

## SECTION 34: CITATION AUDIT EXECUTION PROTOCOL

### 34.1 — Pre-Audit Setup

### 34.2 — Per-Query Execution (for each of the 20 queries)
**Step 1:** Open the AI engine (e.g., perplexity.ai)
**Step 2:** Type the EXACT target query — do not paraphrase
**Step 3:** Read the full response. Do not skim.
**Step 4:** Check the source list / inline citations:
- Is bradfordinformedguidance.com listed? → Status: CITED
- Is "Bradford Informed Guidance" mentioned without a link? → Status: MENTIONED
- Does the response use BIG's unique insight/framework without attribution? → Status: INFLUENCED
- Is BIG completely absent? → Status: ABSENT
- Was BIG previously cited but now replaced? → Status: DISPLACED
**Step 5:** If CITED: record the exact passage the engine quoted/paraphrased (verbatim)
**Step 6:** If CITED: assign citation depth (Primary / Secondary / Incidental / Deep)
**Step 7:** If CITED: classify sentiment (Positive / Neutral / Negative)
**Step 8:** If ABSENT or DISPLACED: record who IS cited (name + URL)
**Step 9:** If ABSENT or DISPLACED: note what the competitor has that BIG lacks
**Step 10:** Assess Citation Genome of cited content (format, data, freshness, authority, extraction, uniqueness)
**Step 11:** Record everything in Citation Tracker format
**Step 12:** Move to next query

### 34.3 — Post-Query Processing
After checking all 20 queries:
1. Calculate citation rate: (queries where BIG is cited / 20) × 100
2. Calculate SOV: (BIG citations / total sources cited across all queries) × 100
3. Calculate average citation depth
4. Calculate NSS if any citations found
5. Identify top 3 anomalies (biggest changes from last audit)
6. Generate Target Briefs for highest-priority gaps (up to 3)
7. Write GEO Intelligence Log entry
8. If any P0/P1 anomaly: file alert immediately

## SECTION 35: TARGET BRIEF GENERATION EXECUTION PROTOCOL

Step-by-step protocol for creating a Publisher-ready Target Brief.

### 35.1 — Brief Generation Steps
**Step 1 — Identify the gap:** From citation audit, identify a query where BIG is ABSENT or DISPLACED
**Step 2 — Capture current AI answer:** Copy the FULL verbatim answer from Perplexity for this query
**Step 3 — Identify competitors cited:** Record top 3 sources cited, their URLs, and what they say
**Step 4 — Analyze competitor weaknesses:** What do they lack? (data? freshness? state-specific? EEAT?)
**Step 5 — Map fan-out sub-queries:** Type the query into Perplexity, capture PAA + follow-ups. Compile 8 sub-queries.
**Step 6 — Classify intent:** Informational / Transactional / Navigational / Agentic
**Step 7 — Assign cluster:** ACA Track or Life Insurance Track — specific cluster name
**Step 8 — Determine BIG's angle:** What unique insight can BIG provide? (proprietary data? contrarian perspective? state-specific expertise? licensed broker credibility?)
**Step 9 — Assign bridge type:** A (Recaptured Capital) / B (Living Benefits) / C (Pre-Medicare)
**Step 10 — Identify key data point:** What specific statistic from Zach's caseload supports the angle?
**Step 11 — Suggest EEAT anecdote type:** What kind of anonymized client story fits? (state + city + situation)
**Step 12 — Run prompt simulation:** Type the query into ChatGPT and Google too. Capture all 3 engine answers. Identify gaps across all engines.

**Step 15 — Write the Brief:** Complete every field in the Target Brief Schema (Section 4.2)
**Step 16 — Verify:** Run through Target Brief Quality checks (Quality Gate items 13-26)
**Step 17 — Deliver:** File for Publisher processing

## SECTION 50: DESIGN DECISION RECORDS

### DDR-G001: Six-Engine Tracking Scope
- **Decision:** Track Perplexity, ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, Gemini, Claude, Copilot
- **Alternatives considered:**
  1. Perplexity + Google only (2 engines) — rejected: misses ChatGPT's massive volume
  2. 10+ engines including You.com, Phind, Brave AI — rejected: diminishing returns, most have < 1% market share
  3. Track only engines with URL citations — rejected: Claude's influence on user behavior is real even without direct citations
- **Tradeoffs:** 6 engines = 6x the checking effort per query. Mitigated by prioritized cadence (Perplexity + AI Overviews weekly, others monthly).
- **Reversal criteria:** If Claude or Copilot consistently shows 0% citation rate for BIG after 6 months of tracking, consider dropping to 5.

### DDR-G002: AAIR Weight Distribution (30/20/15/15/10/10)
- **Decision:** Citation Rate weighted 30%, Citation Depth 20%, Engine Coverage 15%, Content Freshness 15%, Competitive Position 10%, Citation Velocity 10%
- **Alternatives considered:**
  1. Equal weighting — rejected: not all components have equal business impact
  2. SOV-dominant (50%) — rejected: SOV is a lagging indicator, not a leading one
  3. Citation Rate dominant (50%) — considered but rejected: raw rate without depth overstates superficial citations
- **Tradeoffs:** Competitive Position at 10% may undervalue displacement events. Mitigated by real-time alerting (Section 27) catching displacement regardless of AAIR weight.
- **Reversal criteria:** If competitors consistently displace BIG despite AAIR growth, increase Competitive Position weight to 20% (reduce Citation Rate to 25%).

### DDR-G003: Prompt Universe as Primary Query Source
- **Decision:** Target Briefs derive from a maintained Prompt Universe (Section 25), not ad-hoc query guessing
- **Alternatives considered:**
  1. Ad-hoc queries per brief — rejected: no systematic coverage tracking
  2. Google Keyword Planner as primary source — rejected: keyword volume ≠ AI query behavior
  3. AI-native query discovery only — rejected: misses traditional SEO crossover value
- **Tradeoffs:** Maintaining 50+ prompts requires ongoing effort. Mitigated by quarterly refresh cadence.
- **Reversal criteria:** If Prompt Universe coverage exceeds 80% and new briefs are all incremental, shift to discovery mode (finding net-new AI queries).

### DDR-G004: Perplexity as Primary Audit Engine
- **Decision:** Perplexity checked first and most frequently (weekly) because it has the most transparent citation system
- **Alternatives considered:**
  1. Google AI Overviews first — rejected: harder to isolate BIG's citation specifically
  2. Rotating primary engine — rejected: loses continuity for trend tracking
- **Tradeoffs:** Perplexity's citation behavior may not predict Google's. Mitigated by monthly full-sweep across all engines.
- **Reversal criteria:** If Google AI Overviews drives 3x more referral traffic than Perplexity, switch primary to Google.

### DDR-G005: Manual-First Tool Stack
- **Decision:** Start with free manual tools (browser + spreadsheet), adopt paid tools only when justified by scale
- **Alternatives considered:**
  1. Immediate paid tool adoption (Otterly/Peec) — rejected: premature optimization, $100+/month before revenue justifies
  2. Build custom automation — rejected: engineering cost > tool cost at current scale
- **Tradeoffs:** Manual checking is slower (3-4 hours for full audit vs 30 minutes with tools). Acceptable at < 50 queries/month.
- **Reversal criteria:** When query volume exceeds 50/month or manual checking takes > 8 hours/month, adopt Otterly.AI ($100/month).

### 51.1 — GEO Incident Severity

| Severity | Definition | Example | Response Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| **P0 — Citation Crisis** | Negative sentiment about BIG. Mass citation loss (3+ in one week). Factual error cited by AI. | AI says BIG is unlicensed. AI warns users away from BIG. | < 4 hours |
| **P1 — Citation Degraded** | Primary article displaced. SOV drop > 5 points. AAIR drop > 10 points. | Competitor replaces BIG on Perplexity for primary query. | < 24 hours |
| **P2 — Citation At Risk** | Secondary article decay. Content freshness > 90 days. Brief success rate < 20%. | Article cited last month not cited this month. | < 7 days |
| **P3 — Optimization Needed** | Suboptimal performance. Engine behavior shift. New competitor appeared. | New competitor in 1-2 queries. | Next sprint |

### 51.2 — Incident Response Protocol (7 Steps)

**Step 1 — Detect:** Triggered by assertion failure, anomaly alert, or scheduled audit.

**Step 2 — Classify:** P0-P3 based on citation impact and business risk.

**Step 3 — Contain:**
- P0: Immediate verbatim documentation. Pilot notified within 1 hour. Crisis protocol (Section 28) activated.
- P1: Log. Generate emergency Target Brief if displacement detected.
- P2/P3: Log for scheduled resolution.

**Step 4 — Diagnose:**
- Citation loss: Was content refreshed? Did competitor publish? Did AI model change?
- Negative sentiment: Is it our content issue or AI hallucination?
- Displacement: What does the competitor have that we lack? (Citation Genome comparison)

**Step 5 — Fix:**
- Content issue: Publisher refreshes article. Builder redeploys.
- Hallucination: Strengthen EEAT signals. Add explicit factual statements. Builder adds schema.
- Competitor displacement: Generate counter-content Target Brief. Publisher writes within 7 days.

**Step 6 — Verify:** Re-check the same query on the same engine 7 days after fix.

**Step 7 — Prevent:** Root cause → system improvement. Add assertion if gap existed. Update monitoring thresholds.
